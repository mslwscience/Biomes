🌱 Part 1 — What Are Abiotic Factors? ~8 min
📖
Every ecosystem has two types of factors that shape it. Biotic factors are the living things — plants, animals, fungi, bacteria. Abiotic factors are the non-living things — but they control WHERE living things can survive. Think of abiotic factors as the rules of the game that every organism must follow.
💡
The word abiotic comes from Greek: a = without, bios = life. So abiotic = without life. Together, abiotic factors determine which biome forms in a region — and which organisms can live there.

Biome

Climate
The long-term average of weather in an area — including temperature and precipitation patterns over many years.

Precipitation
Water that falls from the atmosphere — rain, snow, sleet, or hail. One of the two most important abiotic factors for determining biomes.

Salinity
The amount of dissolved salt in water. Freshwater has very low salinity; ocean water has high salinity. Controls what aquatic organisms can survive.
👆 Tap to reveal
pH
A measure of how acidic or basic soil or water is. Scale 0-14; 7 is neutral. Most plants need soil between pH 6-7. Very acidic or very basic conditions limit which organisms survive.
👆 Tap to reveal
Topography
The shape and features of the land — mountains, valleys, slopes, elevation. Topography changes local temperature, precipitation, and sunlight exposure.
👆 Tap to reveal
Carrying Capacity
The largest population an environment can support. Abiotic factors set the limit — less water or food = lower carrying capacity = fewer organisms can survive.

🤯 Fun Fact!
Amazon rainforest soils are actually nutrient-poor — most nutrients are locked up inside living plants and animals! When a tree dies, other plants absorb those nutrients incredibly fast before they wash away. The rainforest is rich in life, not in soil!
🌡️🌧️ Part 3 — The Big Two: Temperature & Precipitation ~10 min
⭐
While all 8 abiotic factors matter, temperature and precipitation are the two most powerful forces in determining which biome forms in a region. Scientists use these two factors to classify and predict biomes all over Earth. If you know a place's average temperature AND how much rain it gets, you can predict what biome it is!

🌡️ Temperature — The First Big Factor

🌡️
Temperature controls:
• How fast organisms grow and reproduce (enzyme activity)
• Whether plants can photosynthesize year-round or must go dormant
• Animal behaviors: hibernation, migration, daily activity patterns
• Whether water falls as rain or snow
• How long the growing season is

☁️ Cold Temperatures

  • ❌Slow enzyme activity → slow growth
  • ❌Short or no growing season
  • ❌Water freezes → unavailable to plants
  • ✔Animals adapt: thick fur, hibernation, migration
  • 📍Example: Tundra — only mosses and tiny shrubs

☀️ Warm Temperatures

  • ✔Fast enzyme activity → rapid growth
  • ✔Long or year-round growing season
  • ✔Liquid water available longer
  • ✔High plant diversity and density
  • 📍Example: Tropical rainforest — trees grow year-round

🌧️ Precipitation — The Second Big Factor

🌧️
Precipitation controls:
• Water availability for plants to photosynthesize and grow
• Whether trees, grasses, or only succulents can survive
• How frequent wildfires are (dry = more fires)
• Soil moisture and which nutrients plants can absorb
• The carrying capacity of the ecosystem

☁️ Low Precipitation (Dry)

  • ❌Very few plants can survive
  • ❌Low biodiversity
  • ✔Drought-adapted plants: cacti, succulents
  • 🔥More frequent wildfires
  • 📍Example: Desert — less than 250mm/year

🌧️ High Precipitation (Wet)

  • ✔Dense, multi-layered forests possible
  • ✔Very high biodiversity
  • ✔Year-round plant growth
  • ✔Rich food webs
  • 📍Example: Tropical rainforest — 200cm+/year

📈 Temperature + Precipitation Together = Biome

🤔
Scientists discovered that if you know BOTH temperature AND precipitation for a place, you can predict its biome. Look at this chart — it shows how the two factors combine to produce different biomes!
Temperature Precipitation Biome Produced Example
🔥 Hot year-round 🌧 Very High (200cm+) 🌴 Tropical Rainforest Amazon, Brazil
🔥 Warm ☀ Very Low (<25cm) 🌳 Hot Desert Sonoran, Arizona
🌿 Warm with seasons ☔ Moderate, seasonal 🌾 Savanna / Grassland East Africa, Montana
🌿 Moderate (4 seasons) ☔ Moderate, year-round 🍂 Temperate Deciduous Forest Ohio, Eastern USA
❄ Cold ☔ Moderate 🌲 Boreal / Taiga Forest Canada, Russia
❄❄ Extremely Cold ☀ Very Low 🦗 Tundra Alaska, Arctic
🤯 Did you know?
You can actually have a cold desert! Antarctica is the world's largest desert — not because it is hot, but because it receives less than 20 cm of precipitation per year. The key to desert is dryness, not heat!

🌎 Part 4 — Real-World Examples ~10 min
👀
Now let's look at three real biomes and identify exactly which abiotic factors make each one unique. As you read, look for how temperature and precipitation are the main drivers — with other factors adding detail.

🌳 Case Study 1: Sonoran Desert, Arizona

T
Temperature: Very hot summers (often 40°C+), mild winters (~10-15°C). The wide range matters — plants must survive both extremes.
P
Precipitation: Only about 30 cm (12 inches) per year — and it comes in two unreliable pulses. Months can go by with zero rain.
🥰
Soil: Sandy, low in organic matter, drains rapidly. Water disappears fast after rain — plants must absorb it instantly.
🌳
Result: Only specially adapted organisms survive — saguaro cacti (store water in stems), creosote bushes (deep roots), and animals that are mostly active at night to avoid heat.

🌴 Case Study 2: Amazon Rainforest, Brazil

T
Temperature: Consistently warm year-round — about 26-27°C every month. No cold season. Plants can grow 365 days a year.
P
Precipitation: Enormous — 200-400 cm per year. Even the "dry" months still receive 60-90 mm of rain. Water is almost never a limiting factor.
🥰
Soil (surprise!): Actually nutrient-POOR! Rain washes nutrients away quickly. Nearly all nutrients are locked inside living plants and animals — when something dies, it is recycled almost instantly.
🌴
Result: Dense, layered forest. Highest biodiversity on Earth — more than half of all species live here. Rapid nutrient cycling keeps everything alive.

⛰️ Case Study 3: Rocky Mountains — Elevation Effects

⛰️
Topography changes everything: As you climb a mountain, temperature drops about 6°C for every 1,000 meters of elevation gain. The same mountain hosts multiple biomes from base to peak!
1
Base (low elevation): Temperate deciduous forest — warm, moderate rain, deer and oaks.
2
Middle (mid elevation): Boreal/conifer forest — colder, more snow, elk and pines.
3
Near top: Alpine meadow — too cold for trees, only low grasses and wildflowers.
❄
Peak: Tundra-like conditions — freezing, almost no plants, only lichens on rocks.
💡
Key insight: One mountain = four biomes. The only thing that changed was elevation — which changed temperature and precipitation. This proves how powerful these two abiotic factors are!
🤯 Fun Fact!
Some alpine plants in the Rocky Mountains grow so slowly in the cold, short growing season that they can be hundreds of years old and still only be a few centimeters tall! A tiny plant you step on might be older than your grandparents.

🌍 The Big Idea
Abiotic factors are the non-living rules that every living thing must follow. Temperature and precipitation set the stage — they determine which biome forms. Then soil, sunlight, pH, salinity, topography, and disturbance add the details. Change any of these factors significantly, and the whole community of life changes with it.
