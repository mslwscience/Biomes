📊 How to Read a Climatograph

7th Grade Science  ·  Ohio Standard 7LS2  ·  Biomes Unit

Part 1 of 3
📊 Part 1 — What Is a Climatograph? ~5 min
📖
A climatograph (also called a climate graph) is a special chart scientists use to show the climate of a place. It packs TWO types of information into one graph — how much precipitation (rain/snow) falls each month AND how warm or cold the temperature is each month.
💡
By looking at a climatograph, you can figure out what biome a place belongs to — without ever visiting it! A scientist in Ohio can look at a graph from Brazil and know it is a tropical rainforest just from the pattern of bars and the line.

👉 Here is what a climatograph looks like:

Columbus, Ohio — An Example Climatograph
12 months of real climate data — your home state!
Blue bars = Precipitation (mm) ← read LEFT numbers
Red line = Temperature (°C) → read RIGHT numbers
🔍
Notice two things: There are numbers on BOTH sides of the graph. The LEFT side is for the blue bars (precipitation in mm). The RIGHT side is for the red line (temperature in °C). They are completely different scales — do not mix them up!
🔎 Part 2 — How to Read a Climatograph ~7 min
🌟
There are 4 key things to read from any climatograph. Once you master these, you can identify any biome just by looking at its graph!
Columbus, Ohio — Labeled & Annotated
Each number below matches a callout explanation
1
Blue Bars = Precipitation
Each bar shows how much rain or snow fell that month. Read the number on the LEFT side. Taller bar = more rain.
2
Red Line = Temperature
The dot for each month shows the average temperature. Read the number on the RIGHT side. Higher dot = warmer month.
3
Tallest Bar = Rainiest Month
Find the tallest blue bar → that is the month with the most precipitation. In Columbus, June is the rainiest (~101mm).
4
Peak of Line = Hottest Month
Find where the red line is highest → that is the warmest month. In Columbus, July is hottest (~24°C / 75°F).

📋 Quick Reference — 4 Questions to Ask Every Time

Which month has the tallest bar? → That is the rainiest month.
Which month has the shortest bar? → That is the driest month.
Where is the red line highest? → That is the hottest month.
Where is the red line lowest? → That is the coldest month.

📊 Compare Three Patterns Side by Side

👉
Look at the shape of the red line and the pattern of the blue bars in each graph. These shapes tell you everything about a climate!
🍂 Has Seasons
Columbus, Ohio
Red line arches — low in winter, high in summer. That big up-and-down swing means 4 clear seasons.
Blue bars are moderate and steady all year — rain falls in every month.
🌴 No Seasons
Singapore
Red line is flat — barely moves. Temperature is almost the same every month — always warm near the equator.
Blue bars are tall all year — it rains heavily in every month with no real dry season.
🌧 Monsoon
Mumbai, India
Blue bars spike dramatically — nearly zero rain most months, then a massive explosion of rain June-September. That spike IS the monsoon.
Red line stays warm all year — no cold season.
💡 Remember!
Blue bars → LEFT scale (mm / inches of precipitation). Red line → RIGHT scale (°C / °F temperature). They are on opposite sides on purpose — so you always know which number to read!

Tropical Rainforest — Lots of Rain All Year

Example location: Manaus, Brazil (Amazon Rainforest)

Manaus, Brazil — Tropical Rainforest
Near the equator — one of the wettest places on Earth
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

ALL bars are tall — every single month gets a lot of rain. Even the "driest" month (July, ~60mm) is still wetter than many Ohio months!
Rainiest months are Jan–Mar (~250–260 mm each) — that is a LOT. Ohio's rainiest month is only about 112mm.
The red line is flat and high — temperature barely changes. It stays around 26–27°C all year. No cold season, no hot season — just always warm!
Result: Hot + wet all year = perfect conditions for dense forest. This is why the Amazon has more species than anywhere else on Earth.
🌟
Key Pattern: Tall bars every month + flat warm red line = Tropical Rainforest.
🌳

Desert — Very Little Precipitation

Example location: Tucson, Arizona (Sonoran Desert)

Tucson, Arizona — Hot Desert
Home of the saguaro cactus — very dry all year
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

ALL bars are very short — most months get only 3–25mm of rain. Compare these tiny bars to the Amazon's 60–260mm bars!
Two small rain spikes — a little winter rain (Jan–Mar) and a small "monsoon" pulse in July–August. But both are still very small!
The red line goes up and down a lot — January is cool (~11°C) but July hits ~34°C. Big temperature swing between seasons.
Result: Very little water + hot summers = only cacti, succulents, and specially adapted animals can survive here.
🌟
Key Pattern: Short bars all year + red line that changes a lot = Desert. Look for the left scale being very small (max ~100mm).
🔥

Very Hot Climate — High Temperatures All Year

Example location: Riyadh, Saudi Arabia (Hot Desert)

Riyadh, Saudi Arabia — Extreme Heat
One of the hottest cities on Earth — summer temperatures reach 42°C
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

The red line stays very HIGH — even the coolest month (January) is about 14°C. Summer months reach 42°C — that is 107°F!
Red line peaks sharply in summer — the line shoots up to the top of the right scale from June through August.
Bars are tiny or absent — most months get almost zero rain. The total for the whole year is less than 100mm.
Result: Extreme heat + almost no rain = very harsh. Only the most specialized desert organisms survive here.
🌟
Key Pattern: Red line pushed to the very top of the scale = extremely hot climate. When the RIGHT scale goes up to 45°C, you know it is serious heat.
❄

Very Cold Climate — Low Temperatures All Year

Example location: Barrow, Alaska (Arctic Tundra)

Barrow, Alaska — Arctic Tundra
One of the coldest inhabited places on Earth
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

The red line stays BELOW zero most of the year — from October through May, temperatures are below 0°C (freezing). The ground stays frozen!
Only a brief warm period — July and August barely reach above 4–5°C. That is the entire "summer" — only about 8 weeks!
Bars are tiny — very little precipitation falls, and most of it is snow. Total precipitation is less than 120mm per year.
Result: Most of the year is frozen. Only mosses, lichens, and tiny flowering plants can survive. Animals must be specially adapted to extreme cold.
🌟
Key Pattern: Red line below zero for many months + tiny bars = Arctic Tundra. Notice the RIGHT scale goes far negative (like −30°C).
🍂

Has Clear Seasons — Temperature Changes a Lot

Example location: Columbus, Ohio (Temperate Deciduous Forest)

Columbus, Ohio — Temperate Deciduous Forest
4 clear seasons — your home biome!
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

The red line makes a big arch — it starts low in winter (−1°C in January), climbs to a peak in summer (24°C in July), then falls back down. That arch shape = 4 seasons!
Temperature difference is huge — from −1°C to 24°C is a 25-degree swing. That is why Ohio trees lose their leaves in fall to survive the cold winter.
Bars are moderate and steady — it rains every month (57–112mm). No extreme dry season, no extreme wet season. Spread evenly through the year.
Result: Moderate rain + clear 4 seasons = deciduous trees (that drop leaves), seasonal animals (that hibernate or migrate), and a diverse but not tropical community.
🌟
Key Pattern: A big arch in the red line = clear seasons. The bigger the arch, the more dramatic the seasons.
🌴

No Seasons — Temperature Stays Flat All Year

Example location: Singapore (Tropical Equatorial)

Singapore — No Seasons
On the equator — nearly the same temperature every single month
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

The red line is nearly FLAT — temperature stays between 26–28°C every single month. There is almost no change from January to December. No winter, no summer!
Compare to Ohio's arch — Ohio's line swings 25 degrees. Singapore's line barely moves 2 degrees. That flat line is your signal: no seasons here.
Bars are tall all year — lots of rain every month (150–300mm). High rain + constant warmth = lush tropical vegetation year-round.
Result: Flat temperature line + tall bars = tropical with no dry season. Plants grow 365 days a year without ever stopping.
🌟
Key Pattern: Flat red line = no seasons. If the line barely moves across the year, the place is near the equator and stays warm all the time.
🌧

Monsoon Climate — One Extreme Wet Season

Example location: Mumbai, India (Tropical Monsoon)

Mumbai, India — Monsoon Climate
Famous for its dramatic wet season — it either pours or it is dry!
Precipitation (mm) ← LEFT
Temperature (°C) → RIGHT

🔎 What to Notice on This Graph

Bars are EXTREMELY uneven — some months are very dry (Jan–May, less than 20mm) but then suddenly June–September get enormous amounts (400–600mm per month!). This is the monsoon season.
The spike is dramatic — July is the peak at around 617mm. That is more rain in ONE month than Columbus gets in an entire year!
Temperature stays warm but dips during monsoon — the heavy rain actually cools things slightly in July. Temperature is warm all year (24–30°C) with no cold season.
Result: The most recognizable climatograph pattern! Flat or low bars for months, then a sudden enormous spike. The plants and animals are adapted to survive the long dry season AND the flood season.
🌟
Key Pattern: Most months dry, then a sudden HUGE spike in bars for a few months = Monsoon. The spike is so dramatic it is impossible to miss.
💡 Summary — What Each Pattern Means
Tall flat bars all year = tropical wet  |  All bars very short = desert  |  Red line near top of scale = very hot  |  Red line below zero many months = very cold  |  Red line makes a big arch = 4 seasons  |  Red line nearly flat = no seasons  |  Sudden giant bar spike = monsoon
